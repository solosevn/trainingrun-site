Mercury Models: A New Way to Make AI Faster

Diffusion-based models are breaking speed barriers in AI processing.

By TrainingRun.AI March 6, 2026 Compute Infrastructure

Imagine waiting for your phone to load an app, or for a chatbot to respond, and it takes forever. That lag isn’t just annoying—it’s a real problem when AI systems need to handle huge amounts of data quickly, whether for coding, customer service, or even life-saving medical tools.

Today, I want to share something exciting that could change how fast AI works. A group of researchers has developed a new approach using what they call diffusion models to create ultra-fast language models named Mercury Coder Mini and Mercury Coder Small. Unlike the traditional methods most AI systems use, which process data step-by-step in a linear way (called autoregressive processing), these models take a different path. They’ve shown remarkable results, with the Mini model hitting speeds of 1,109 tokens per second on high-powered H100 GPUs, and the Small model not far behind at 737 tokens per second. To put that in perspective, that’s up to ten times faster than some of the best competing models out there right now.

What’s the Problem with Current AI Speed?

Most AI language models today rely on autoregressive processing. Think of it like reading a book word by word, left to right—you can’t move to the next word until you’ve finished the one before. This method works well for accuracy, ensuring the AI understands context, but it’s slow when you’re dealing with massive tasks like writing code or analyzing data. As AI becomes part of more daily tools—think virtual assistants or real-time translation—this slowness becomes a bottleneck. We need systems that can keep up with our pace.

How Mercury Models Solve This

The Mercury models flip the script by using diffusion, a technique that’s more like solving a puzzle by looking at the whole picture at once rather than piece by piece. Diffusion models have been used before in things like image generation (think of those AI tools that create art from text prompts), but applying them to language and coding tasks at this scale is new. The researchers trained these models specifically for coding, and the results are staggering. Not only do they process data much faster, but they still maintain the quality of output—meaning they’re not just quick, they’re effective. For example, imagine a programmer getting near-instant suggestions for code fixes instead of waiting seconds or minutes. That’s the kind of leap we’re talking about.

Why This Matters to You

So, why should you care about some technical breakthrough in AI speed? Because this isn’t just about faster coding tools for tech folks. If diffusion models like Mercury can scale up, they could make every AI-powered service you use—whether it’s a voice assistant, a recommendation engine, or even fraud detection on your bank account—work quicker and more efficiently. For me, as a dad, I think about how this could mean better educational tools for my kids, where learning apps respond instantly to their questions. For businesses, it could cut costs and improve customer experiences. And in critical areas like healthcare, faster AI could mean quicker diagnoses or emergency responses.

There’s still work to do. These models are in the research stage, and scaling them for everyday use will take time and testing. But the potential is clear: we’re moving toward a world where AI doesn’t just think for us—it keeps up with us. I’m excited to see where this leads, because if we can solve the speed problem, we’re one step closer to making technology truly serve us in real time.
